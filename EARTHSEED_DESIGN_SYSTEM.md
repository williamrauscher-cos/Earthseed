# Earthseed: The Books of the Living
## A Design System

*"I'm not interested in being fancy, or even original. Clarity and truth will be plenty, if I can only achieve them."*
— Lauren Oya Olamina

---

## On This Document

This is not a brand guide. It is a **propagation kit**.

Earthseed spreads the way seeds spread — carried by wind and animal, planted in whatever ground is available, adapted to local conditions. The visual system described here is not a set of restrictions. It is a **shared language** — the minimum agreement that makes Earthseed recognizable across communities that may never meet each other, that may be separated by hundreds of miles of dangerous highway, that may have access to very different materials and tools.

A community in a coastal settlement will produce different-looking materials than a community in the Central Valley. That is correct. The doctrine is the same. The language is the same. The production is whatever the community has.

Anyone holding this document has permission to use it. There is no licensing, no central authority, no hierarchy of authenticity. The only test is whether the materials produced serve the doctrine: **God is Change. Shape the Change you can.**

---

## I. Creative Position

*The aesthetic of surviving collapse with your intelligence intact — handmade objects that carry the weight of eternity, made from what the earth provides, legible by firelight, designed for a reader who is walking north.*

Earthseed design is **anti-monumental**. It does not reach for grandeur. Grandeur is what the old world built — and burned, and abandoned. What Earthseed reaches for instead is **clarity at human scale**: a text that one person can read aloud to others gathered around a fire, a mark that can be made with a burned stick or embroidered with salvaged thread, a document that fits inside a pillowcase with everything else a person owns.

At the same time, the content is not small. The Destiny — to take root among the stars — is the most ambitious proposition a religion has ever made. The design holds this tension: **intimate form, cosmic content**. A pocket notebook that contains a claim about the future of the species.

The visual register is not raw or chaotic. It is **disciplined scarcity** — the aesthetic of someone who has read everything and decided that what matters is legibility, survival, and truth. It should look like something a brilliant person made carefully with limited tools. Not accidentally rough. Deliberately spare.

**What this system is not:**

- It is not New Age. No soft gradients, no crystals, no healing-adjacent warmth.
- It is not Christian. No cross, no dove, no gold-leaf reverence.
- It is not African diaspora religious visual culture, though it honors that inheritance. Earthseed is new. It does not borrow iconography from Vodou, Santería, or any other living tradition.
- It is not Afrofuturism as aesthetic genre. It is Afrofuturism as necessity — Black survival intelligence applied to an actual apocalypse.
- It is not designed by someone who has never been hungry.

**Reference coordinates:** Iron gall manuscripts on rough vellum. Zines made on a church photocopier at 2 AM. The *Whole Earth Catalog*, issue one. Stone-cut Roman inscriptions, read two thousand years later. A composition notebook that someone treated as if it mattered.

## II. Doctrine as Design Constraint

The theology is not background context. It is a specification document.

**"Create no images of God. Accept the images that God has provided. They are everywhere, in everything."**

This verse is a design rule. No deity figures. No abstract divine symbol invented for the religion. The permissible image vocabulary is the natural world — because in Earthseed, the natural world *is* the image of God. A seed, an oak, a star field, fire, water: these are not decorative choices. They are theological statements and must be treated with corresponding seriousness.

**"God is neither good nor evil, neither loving nor hating. God is Power. God is Change."**

This prevents visual comfort. Earthseed design does not console. It does not tell you everything will be fine. It tells you that Change is real, that you can shape it or be shaped by it, and that the difference depends on your intelligence and your effort. The visual register holds this — not harshly, but honestly.

**"Belief / Initiates and guides action — / Or it does nothing."**

Every designed object must earn its existence by serving action. Decorative elements that do not serve readability, portability, or community function are in violation of the doctrine. When in doubt, remove it.

**"Intelligence is ongoing, individual adaptability."**

This system is a starting point. Communities are expected to adapt it. The mark may be drawn differently by different hands. The paper may be different. The ink may be different. The doctrine is the constant; the execution adapts to circumstance. This is not a failure of the system — it *is* the system.

## III. Color

The palette is derived from what the earth provides in coastal Northern California — the pigments available without industrial supply chains. Every color has a source material. If you cannot name what made a color, the color does not belong here.

### Primary Palette

| Token | Hex | oklch | Source Material | Role |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| `Soil` | `#1A1209` | `oklch(10% 0.025 50)` | Iron-rich clay, dark earth | Primary text; near-black ground |
| `Ash` | `#EDE6D6` | `oklch(91% 0.018 80)` | Wood ash, fired clay slip | Primary background; warm off-white |
| `Ochre` | `#C17A2A` | `oklch(58% 0.12 60)` | Iron oxide, yellow ochre | Primary accent; fire; change-in-action |

| `Night` | `#0C0D14` | `oklch(7% 0.02 260)` | Night sky, deep indigo | Dark ground for the astronomical register |
| `Star` | `#F0F2FF` | `oklch(96% 0.015 260)` | Starlight, pale phosphor | Text and marks on Night ground |

### Secondary Palette

| Token | Hex | oklch | Source Material | Role |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| `Char` | `#070503` | `oklch(5% 0.01 50)` | Charcoal, lamp black | Maximum contrast; large display text only |
| `Woad` | `#3D5C6E` | `oklch(40% 0.055 230)` | Woad plant, indigo vat | Water; distance; space |

| `Seed` | `#7A8E4F` | `oklch(57% 0.07 130)` | Dried grass, lichen, new growth | Growth; planting; cautious hope |
| `Ember` | `#7A2E0E` | `oklch(30% 0.1 35)` | Deep ember, red clay | Warning; sacrifice; necessary burning |

### Color Rules

1. **Ash is not white.** `#EDE6D6` — warm, slightly tan. Never pure `#FFFFFF`. The warmth is the paper showing through the ink. This distinction is what separates a printed page from a screen.

2. **Soil is not black.** `#1A1209` carries warmth from the clay. Use `Char` only for maximum-contrast display text at large scale — never for body copy or labels.

3. **Ochre is the only accent.** One color earns the right to be noticed. Everything else defers to it. Ochre marks what matters: a key verse, the Earthseed mark, a founding date. Do not dilute its signal by using it decoratively.

4. **Fire is handled carefully.** Ochre is sacred fire — the generative, transforming flame. Ember is destructive fire — what must burn for the phoenix to rise. They are not interchangeable. Ember should be used rarely and with intentionality, never for welcoming materials.

5. **Night + Star is the astronomical register.** Reserved for materials that address the Destiny directly — the long arc toward the stars. Do not use Night ground for community notices, gathering programs, or practical documents. It is for materials that look forward across generations.

6. **No synthetic-feeling color.** No pure saturated RGB values. No color that could not have been made from a plant, a mineral, or a clay. If a color looks like it came from a screen rather than the earth, it does not belong.

7. **Woad, Bark, Seed, and Ember are contextual.** Use them to support the subject of a specific document, not as defaults. A burial ceremony might use Bark. A planting ceremony might use Seed. A passage about sacrifice might use Ember.

### Color Pairings

| Ground | Primary Text | Accent | Register |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| Ash | Soil | Ochre | Standard — daily documents, broadside, pocket booklet |
| Night | Star | Ochre | Astronomical — Destiny-facing materials, ceremony |
| Ash | Soil | Woad | Water — coastal communities, passage across distance |
| Ash | Soil | Bark | Root — founding documents, burial ceremony, permanence |

## IV. Typography

Two voices. The distinction is doctrinal, not aesthetic.

Earthseed produces two kinds of text: **the verse** — doctrine, written to be memorized, spoken aloud, and tested against lived experience — and **the record** — the journal, the notice, the date, the practical act of keeping track of what happened and when. These are theologically different activities. Lauren kept separate notebooks for each. The typefaces enforce that separation.

### Voice 1 — The Verse

**Typeface:** IM Fell English (free, Google Fonts)
**Fallback:** Cormorant Garamond, then Georgia

**Character:** IM Fell English was digitized from a seventeenth-century typeface made for the Oxford University Press. It carries the imperfections of that origin — slight irregularities in letterform, bracketed serifs with visible weight, a texture that reads as hand-set even at screen resolution. In the world of the novel, where printing is artisanal and paper is scarce, this imperfection is not a flaw. It is authenticity. The verse has been handled.

**Role:** All Earthseed verse. The full title "Earthseed: The Books of the Living." The name of the community. Any text that carries doctrinal weight — that wants to be memorized, that is meant to be read aloud.

**Weights in use:**
- Regular (400) — all verse text
- Italic (400i) — attribution lines only: *by Lauren Oya Olamina*, *EARTHSEED: THE BOOKS OF THE LIVING*

**What this voice is not used for:** dates, navigation, labels, section headers, practical logistics, any text that organizes rather than proclaims.

### Voice 2 — The Record

**Typeface:** Courier Prime (free, Google Fonts)
**Fallback:** Courier New, then system monospace

**Character:** Courier Prime is a refined revival of the typewriter face — the machine that exists at the ceiling of the novel's functional technology. It reads as produced rather than designed: consistent enough to be legible, imperfect enough to carry the weight of something made by hand and mechanism rather than software. In the world of the novel, this is not a retro choice — it is the realistic one. Useful. Durable. Clear at distance. Unmistakably different from the verse voice.

**Role:** Dates. Location names in list form. The attribution line "EARTHSEED: THE BOOKS OF THE LIVING" set small as a label. Navigation within a multi-page document. Meeting notices. Gathering schedules. Any text that serves orientation, organization, or practical communication.

**Weights in use:**
- Regular (400) — all uses. Do not introduce weight variation. The system is weight-minimal by production constraint: you cannot print bold reliably on a hand-press, and you cannot embroider bold reliably onto cloth.

**What this voice is not used for:** verse text, even when quoting a verse inside a practical document. A quoted verse in a meeting notice is still set in The Verse typeface.

### The Division Is Load-Bearing

These voices do not appear in each other's territory. This is not a stylistic preference — it is enforcing a theological distinction that Earthseed makes explicitly: the verse is not the same as the record. One is doctrine; the other is witness. Collapsing them loses the point of keeping separate notebooks.

If The Verse typeface appears on a navigation label: mistake.
If The Record typeface appears inside a verse: mistake.

### Type Scale

| Style Name | Voice | Size | Line Height | Letter Spacing | Use |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| `Display Verse` | Verse | 64px | 70px | −0.5px | Large hanging; single-verse broadside |
| `Section Verse` | Verse | 42px | 52px | −0.3px | Cover verse; chapter epigraph |
| `Body Verse` | Verse | 26px | 40px | 0 | Verse in a document read at a table |
| `Pocket Verse` | Verse | 19px | 30px | 0 | Verse in a pocket booklet; spoken-together close |
| `Attribution` | Verse Italic | 15px | 22px | 0 | *by Lauren Oya Olamina*; italic attributions |
| `Label` | Record | 12px | 16px | 2.5px | All-caps; date, location, category — primary label |
| `Label Small` | Record | 11px | 15px | 2px | All-caps; secondary labels, footers, scale captions |
| `Body Record` | Record | 15px | 26px | 0.2px | Meeting notes; order of ceremony notes; practical text |
| `Order Title` | Verse | 21px | 26px | 0 | Named items within an order of ceremony |

*Note: These are screen-calibrated working sizes derived from built artifacts. For print production, scale proportionally from your output format — a half-letter ceremony program at 96dpi uses these values directly.*

### Typography Rules

1. **The verse typeface is for the sacred register only.** Not for schedules, not for headings in a practical document, not for the Earthseed mark set as a wordmark. Only for text that carries doctrinal weight.

2. **The record typeface is for the practical register only.** Even when a verse is quoted inside a practical document — a meeting notice that opens with a relevant verse — the verse is set in The Verse typeface at Body Verse scale, then the practical content resumes in The Record typeface.

3. **Line breaks in verse are doctrinal.** Do not reflow verse text to fill a container. The line breaks are part of the meaning — Lauren wrote them that way. If a container cannot hold the verse at its required breaks, reduce the type size or increase the container. Do not reflow.

4. **Record labels are set in all-caps with letter-spacing.** This is the only available form of emphasis within The Record voice. No weight variation, no color, no underline.

5. **Verse text achieves emphasis through placement and scale, not weight.** A verse set larger is more important. A verse set alone on a page, with nothing else, is sacred. Do not reach for bold.

6. **Dates follow Lauren's format:** `SATURDAY, JULY 31, 2027` — Record typeface, Display Label style, all-caps, at the top-left of every document that is not a large hanging. Every Earthseed document is dated. Every record of Change is located in time.

7. **No centered verse above three lines.** Short one-line or two-line verses may be centered for emphasis on a broadside. Longer verses are set ragged-right, left-aligned. The verse is not a decoration; it is a statement. Statements stand their ground on the left margin.

## V. The Earthseed Mark

### System Mark vs. Community Mark

The design system carries two levels of mark:

**The System Mark** (Earthseed-wide) is the seed-with-arc described in the original propagation kit — a filled ellipse with a single ascending curve, representing the germination instant. Any community may use it.

**The Community Mark** is specific to each community. It takes the community's name as its subject and is developed from the natural image vocabulary. The Acorn Glyph below is the mark of the Acorn community at Bankole's land, Northern California, founded October 2027. It is documented here as the first worked example; other communities should develop their own.

---

### The System Mark (Earthseed-wide)

A filled ellipse — the seed — with a single open arc rising from above it, curving slightly rightward. The arc does not complete a circle. It is in motion: the germination instant, the moment Change becomes visible.

**Construction (for hand-drawing):**
1. Draw a small filled ellipse, wider than tall.
2. From just above its top center, draw a single arc that rises and leans right, ending open. Length roughly 1.5× the ellipse height.
3. That is the complete mark.

**Reduced versions:** At small scale (below 20px), drop the arc and use the ellipse alone. For burned or embroidered applications, simplify the arc to a straight ascending line.

---

### The Acorn Community Mark

The Acorn Glyph is a three-part object: **stem, cap, body**.

- **Stem:** A short vertical stroke, rounded at top, emerging from the center of the cap. Drawn before the cap so it tucks behind it rather than reading as a separate spike.
- **Cap:** A flattened arc in Ochre — wider than a semicircle, pressing down over the body like a hat brim. No texture lines. The silhouette is the cap.
- **Body:** A filled ellipse below the cap, taller than wide. On dark grounds: Ash fill. On light grounds: Soil fill.

The glyph reads at sign scale (gate, exterior wall), document scale (broadside, letterhead), and stamp scale (trade mark, wax seal). It works in one color when necessary — the three-part structure is legible even when cap and body are the same value.

**What was removed in development:** Texture lines across the cap face were cut after testing — they read as upward-shooting spikes rather than surface detail and created ambiguity about the object's orientation. The mark is cleaner without them. This is worth noting for communities developing their own marks: test at multiple scales and remove anything that doesn't survive reduction.

**Construction (for hand-drawing):**
1. Draw a short vertical stroke — the stem.
2. Below it, draw a wide flat arc that curves down and inward at both ends, meeting the body — the cap. Fill it Ochre.
3. Below the cap, draw a vertical ellipse — the body. Fill it with the ground color (Ash on dark, or leave as negative space on stamp/carve applications).

**Color applications:**

| Background | Cap | Body | Stem | Context |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| Soil (dark) | Ochre | Ash | Ochre | Sign; gate; night-facing |
| Ash (light) | Ochre | Soil | Ochre | Document; broadside |
| Ash (light) | Soil | Soil | Soil | Stamp; single-color print |
| Natural cloth | Natural dye / burned | Negative space | Same as cap | Embroidery; pyrography |

**Wordmark pairing:**

```
[Acorn Glyph]

  ACORN
  Earthseed Community
```

"ACORN" in Record typeface, Label style — all-caps, spaced. "Earthseed Community" in Verse typeface, Attribution style — italic, smaller. The glyph sits above both lines, centered. On dark grounds, the wordmark inherits Star or Ash color; labels drop to Dust.

## VI. Natural Image Vocabulary

Per doctrine: *"Create no images of God. Accept the images that God has provided."*

The following images are not decorative choices. They are images of God as Earthseed understands it, and carry theological weight accordingly.

### Permitted Images and Their Doctrinal Ground

| Image | Doctrinal Verse | Context |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Acorn / falling seed | *"Seed to tree, tree to forest"* | Community name; germination; beginning |
| Oak canopy silhouette | *"Tree to forest"*; burial *"Trees are better than stone — life commemorating life"* | Permanence; memorial; community established |
| Root system | *"Take root"* — the Destiny metaphor | Community in ground; belonging; foundation |
| River / water flowing | *"Rain to river, river to sea"* | Change visible; passage; survival |
| Star field, horizon to zenith | *"The Destiny of Earthseed is to take root among the stars"* | The long arc; the reason for endurance |
| Fire / flame — controlled | *"God is both creative and destructive"* | Transformation; the Gathering fire; sacred |
| Horizon line — road going north | The journey north; what cannot yet be seen | Aspiration; the walk; the future |
| Seed dispersal in wind | The spread of Earthseed by human contact | Propagation; the mission |
| Hands — open, not clasped | *"With forethought and work, we shape God"* | Action; agency; community labor |

### What Is Not in the Vocabulary

- Human figures in poses of worship, supplication, or prayer (doctrine: *"We do not worship God. We perceive and attend God."*)
- A central divine figure of any kind
- Doves, halos, auras, light-rays emanating from a center point
- Stars arranged in human-defined shapes (pentagrams, the Big Dipper used as a symbol)
- Abstract sacred geometry borrowed from other traditions
- Any image associated with a specific living religious tradition

### Production Reality

In the world of the novel, photography is unavailable for most community use. Images are produced by:

- Woodblock or linocut relief printing
- Hand-drawing with carbon ink or charcoal
- Embroidery or appliqué on cloth
- Resist-dyeing on natural fiber
- Burning (pyrography) on wood
- Carved or incised marks on stone, wood, or clay

The image vocabulary must work in all of these. When designing an image for an Earthseed document, ask: *Can this be cut into a block of wood? Can this be embroidered?* If not, it is too complex.

Photographic reproduction, where available, is treated as a documentary register — not a sacred one. A photograph of the community planting their first oak trees belongs in the record, not on the wall of the gathering space.

## VII. Physical Formats

Four canonical formats, derived from what can be produced and carried in the world of the novel.

### Format 1: The Pocket Booklet (A6 / 4.1″ × 5.8″)

**This is the original Book of the Living.**

Lauren's first Earthseed notebook was a school composition book. The pocket booklet is its descendant — small enough to fit in a pack alongside everything else a person carries, durable enough for repeated handling, and light enough that a person walking north will not leave it behind.

**Contents:** A curated selection of verses organized by theme or occasion, the Destiny statement, the community's name and founding date. Optionally: blank pages at the back for a new reader's own notes.

**Production:** Saddle-stitched or long-stitched with waxed thread. Cover on slightly heavier stock. Recto/verso printing when paper is available; recto-only when it is not.

**Grid:**
- Margins: 10mm all sides
- Single column
- Baseline grid: 4mm
- No gutter (single column)

**Typography:** Verses at Pocket Verse scale. Dates and community name at Display Label scale. Nothing else.

**Page structure:**
- P.1 (Cover): Mark + wordmark + community name + date
- P.2 (Inside front): The opening verse: *All that you touch / You Change…*
- Interior pages: Verses, one per page or two related verses per spread, with a thin horizontal rule (Dust color, 0.5pt) as the only divider between them
- Final page (Inside back): The Destiny statement, set at Section Verse scale, alone on the page
- P.last (Back cover): Date of first printing; location; *"Copy freely. Adapt to your ground."*

### Format 2: The Broadside (Letter / 8.5″ × 11″ or A4)

**The document for posting and passing.**

A single large sheet, printed or hand-lettered on one side. Contains one verse at large scale — the verse the community has chosen to speak to whoever finds this sheet. The broadside is the document left on a road, nailed to a wall, passed between travelers, tucked into the pocket of someone who asked what Earthseed is.

**Grid:**
- Margins: 22mm all sides
- Single column for verse presentations
- Optional two-column for gathering notices: verse on the left, logistics on the right (split at 60/40)

**Typography:** Verse at Section Verse or Display Verse scale, depending on verse length. Date at Display Label scale, top-left. Community name at Display Label scale, bottom-left. Mark at bottom-right.

**The verse selection discipline:** The verse on a broadside should be legible to someone walking past it at arm's distance. Test readability at 18″. If a line wraps in a way that loses the verse's rhythm, choose a shorter verse or increase the margin.

### Format 3: The Ceremony Program (Half-letter / 5.5″ × 8.5″, folded)

**The document for the founding ritual, the burial, the naming.**

A single letter sheet, folded in half to create four panels. Used for occasions that require a shared order of events — the burial of the dead, the naming of a new community, the formal founding ceremony at Acorn.

**Panel layout:**
- **Cover (outside front):** Mark + occasion name + date + location. Occasion name set in Section Verse scale.
- **Inside left:** The verse selected for this occasion. Set at Body Verse scale, with generous vertical centering — the verse is the altar.
- **Inside right:** The order of the ceremony. Set in Body Record and Display Label — Record voice throughout. Structure: Opening / Verse (title only, spoken by all) / Speaking / Silence / Planting or other closing act.
- **Back (outside back):** Community name. Date of the community's founding (if different from the ceremony). One line only: *"God is Change."*

### Format 4: The Large Hanging (24″ × 36″ or improvised)

**The verse at gathering scale.**

A single verse, readable from fifteen feet. Nothing else.

The hanging is for the gathering space — the wall where the community comes together. It is not a poster with information. It is the verse the community has chosen to live under, selected because it speaks to where they are right now.

**Grid:** 5% of shortest dimension as margin, all sides. The verse runs to full available width within that margin.

**Typography:** Display Verse scale as the minimum. Scale up to fill the available surface. Centered composition is permitted here — the only format where centering is the default, because the hanging is an altar object, not a communication document.

**Verse selection:** Choose a verse that can be absorbed in one standing moment. *"God is Change."* alone on a surface twice the height of a person. *"Kindness eases Change."* The verse should require no context. It should give what it gives immediately.

**Production:** Paper, cloth, wood, plaster — whatever the community has. On cloth, the verse may be embroidered, resist-dyed, or block-printed. On wood, burned or carved. The verse does not require paper.

## IX. Materials and Production

These specifications exist because Earthseed materials are made, not manufactured. The design system must translate into actual production, including production under constraint.

### Paper

**Standard:** Uncoated, cream or natural tone, 70–90gsm. Not bright white — the warmth of the paper is part of the visual register. The eye should rest on it.

**Pocket Booklet:** 80gsm text, 100gsm cover. Saddle-stitched or long-stitched with waxed thread.

**Broadside:** 80–100gsm. The grain of the paper should run parallel to the long dimension, so it folds cleanly if folded for transport.

**When commercial paper is unavailable:** Pressed plant-fiber sheets. Scraped and treated bark. Tightly-woven linen. Smoothed wood. The design system works on all of them. The surface changes; the verse does not.

### Ink

**Standard:** Carbon-based — carbon black, lamp black. The most durable and most available ink without industrial supply chains. It does not fade with UV exposure and can be produced locally from burned organic matter suspended in water with a binder (gum arabic, animal glue).

**Ochre accent:** Natural ochre pigment in linseed oil or gum arabic solution. Alternatively: red clay mixed with water and a binding agent, or turmeric in suspension (less permanent, but available).

**Printing methods in order of infrastructure required:**

1. Hand-lettering with a reed pen, improvised nib, or brush
2. Carved wood stamp for the mark; hand-lettering for text
3. Woodblock or linocut for the full image; hand-lettering or stamp for text
4. Woodblock or linocut for both image and text (requires carving skill and time)
5. Screen printing with a burned-in or cut stencil (requires screen, squeegee, and light source)
6. Risograph (requires electricity, the machine, and masters — but produces the right visual character)
7. Offset printing (highest quality, highest infrastructure requirement)

The design system is built to look correct at method 1 and progressively more refined at methods 2–7. A hand-lettered broadside is not a lesser version of an offset-printed one. It is a different artifact of the same system.

### Cloth

Large hangings and community identity materials may be made in cloth rather than paper. Cloth is more durable for permanent installation and more portable for a community in motion.

**Techniques:**
- Embroidery (the mark; a single verse; the community name)
- Block-printing with natural pigment on cotton or linen
- Resist-dyeing (batik, tie-dye) for ground color variation
- Appliqué for the mark on banners or garments

**Natural dye palette:**
- Ochre / yellow: onion skins (yellow-gold), weld plant (brighter yellow), or iron oxide applied as pigment
- Dark ground: iron mordant on tannin-rich fiber (walnut hull, oak gall); woad or indigo for the blue-black of Night
- Rust/brown: madder root, walnut hull, iron water
- Grey: iron mordant on undyed fiber

## XI. Guardrails

### What to protect in every iteration

1. **The two-voice distinction.** Verse typeface for doctrine; Record typeface for everything else. Non-negotiable.

2. **Line breaks in verse.** Never reflow. Reduce type size instead.

3. **The mark's simplicity.** If the mark cannot be drawn by hand in ten seconds by someone who has seen it once, it has become too complex.

4. **The date.** Every document is dated. Earthseed is not timeless; it is always responding to what is happening now.

5. **The absence of decoration.** If an element does not serve readability, portability, or community function, remove it.

### What to adapt freely

6. **The paper, the ink, the production method.** Use what you have.

7. **The verse selection.** Choose what speaks to where your community is.

8. **The scale.** A booklet made for a community of eight looks different from one made for a community of eighty. The system scales.

9. **The color.** If Ochre iron oxide is unavailable, use what you can make. The concept (one accent color, earth-derived) is the rule; the specific color is an adaptation.

10. **New verses.** The Books of the Living are not closed. Communities that compose new verses mark them with their author's name. Lauren's verses carry the attribution *EARTHSEED: THE BOOKS OF THE LIVING by Lauren Oya Olamina*. New verses carry their own attribution. Both are Earthseed.

### What is prohibited

11. **No images of a deity.** The doctrine is explicit.

12. **No borrowed iconography from other living religious traditions.** Earthseed is new. It does not dress itself in the symbols of what came before.

13. **No comfort that is not honest.** The design does not promise what the doctrine cannot deliver. God does not love you the way a parent loves a child. God is Change. The design tells the truth.

14. **No ornament for its own sake.** This is both an aesthetic position and a doctrinal one. Every element of every document should be able to answer: *Why are you here? What work are you doing?*

## XII. A Note on Adaptation

This document will be copied by hand, printed imperfectly, modified by communities we will never meet. Some copies will drift from what is written here. Verses will be slightly wrong. The mark will look different. A community will find a local pigment that reads differently from Ochre.

All of this is correct.

**God is Change. This document is part of God. It will change.**

The only anchor is the doctrine itself — the verses, the Destiny, the understanding that we are the life that perceives itself changing. If those are intact, the design is doing its work. If the design is beautiful but the doctrine is lost, the design has failed.

*Copy freely. Adapt to your ground. Take root.*
